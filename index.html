The Codebtor Stay

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 1301 -- the provision unique to Chapter 13 that extends the automatic stay to cosigners, co-borrowers, and guarantors on consumer debts.

What Is the Codebtor Stay?

When a debtor files Chapter 13 bankruptcy, the automatic stay under Section 362 stops creditors from collecting against the debtor. But what about the people who cosigned or guaranteed the debtor's loans? Without additional protection, creditors could simply turn around and pursue the cosigner instead.

Section 1301 solves this problem. It extends the automatic stay to any individual who is liable with the debtor on a consumer debt -- meaning cosigners, co-borrowers, and guarantors are also protected from collection while the Chapter 13 case is active. This protection is unique to Chapter 13. It does not exist in Chapter 7 or Chapter 11.

The codebtor stay takes effect automatically when the Chapter 13 case is filed. No separate motion is required. It applies to all consumer debts where someone else shares liability with the debtor, and it remains in place unless the court lifts it on a creditor's motion.

Who Is Protected?

The codebtor stay protects any individual who is liable with the debtor on a consumer debt. This includes:

Cosigners -- a parent who cosigned a car loan, a friend who cosigned a personal loan, or a relative who cosigned a credit card application. If the debtor files Chapter 13, the cosigner is shielded from collection on that debt.

Co-borrowers -- two people who jointly took out a loan. If one files Chapter 13, Section 1301 protects the other from collection activity during the case.

Guarantors -- someone who guaranteed repayment of the debtor's consumer obligation. The guarantee does not make them a debtor, but Section 1301 still protects them.

There are important limits. The codebtor stay only applies to consumer debts -- debts incurred primarily for personal, family, or household purposes. It does not apply to business debts. And it only protects individuals, not corporations or other entities that may share liability.

Why It Matters

Without Section 1301, filing Chapter 13 could create a painful situation: the debtor gets breathing room, but the cosigner gets hammered. A parent who cosigned a car loan could face aggressive collection calls and lawsuits the moment their child files bankruptcy. A spouse who co-signed a credit card could be sued while the other spouse reorganizes through Chapter 13.

The codebtor stay prevents this outcome. It ensures that filing Chapter 13 does not simply shift the collection pressure from the debtor to the people who helped them. This is one of the key reasons debtors with cosigned consumer debts choose Chapter 13 over Chapter 7 -- Chapter 13 protects the cosigner, Chapter 7 does not.

For many debtors, this protection is not just a legal technicality. It is the difference between preserving a family relationship and destroying one. When a parent cosigns a loan out of love and the borrower falls on hard times, Section 1301 keeps the creditor from turning that act of generosity into a financial disaster for the cosigner.

When the Codebtor Stay Can Be Lifted

The codebtor stay is not absolute. Under Section 1301(c), a creditor can ask the court to lift the stay as to the codebtor. The court must grant relief if one of three conditions is met:

1. The codebtor received the consideration. If the codebtor -- not the debtor -- actually received the benefit of the loan, the stay can be lifted. For example, if a parent took out a loan in their name but the child cosigned it, and the parent received all the loan proceeds, the creditor can seek relief from the codebtor stay as to the child. The logic is that the stay is meant to protect people who helped the debtor, not people who were the primary beneficiaries.

2. The plan does not propose to pay the claim. If the debtor's Chapter 13 plan does not propose to pay the codebtor claim, the creditor can seek relief. The codebtor stay is tied to the promise that the debt will be addressed through the plan. If the plan ignores the debt entirely, the creditor should not be prevented from pursuing the cosigner.

3. The creditor's interest would be irreparably harmed. If the creditor can show that its interest would be irreparably harmed by continuation of the stay, the court may lift it. This is a higher bar and requires the creditor to demonstrate actual, concrete harm -- not just inconvenience or delay.

To seek relief, the creditor must file a motion with the bankruptcy court. The debtor and codebtor have the right to respond and object. The court holds a hearing and decides whether relief is warranted.

Chapter 13 Plan Treatment

The codebtor stay works hand-in-hand with the Chapter 13 plan. If the plan proposes to pay the codebtor claim in full -- 100 cents on the dollar -- the cosigner is fully protected for the life of the case. The creditor receives payment through the plan, and the cosigner owes nothing.

If the plan proposes to pay less than the full amount of the codebtor claim, the situation is more complicated. The codebtor stay remains in effect during the case, but the cosigner may be liable for the unpaid balance after the case concludes. The debtor receives a discharge of the remaining balance, but the cosigner does not -- the cosigner's liability is not affected by the debtor's bankruptcy discharge.

This is why attorneys often advise debtors to pay codebtor claims in full through the Chapter 13 plan whenever possible. Full payment through the plan eliminates any residual liability for the cosigner and avoids post-case collection problems.

Related Protections

The codebtor stay under Section 1301 is part of a broader framework of debtor and third-party protections in the Bankruptcy Code:

The Automatic Stay (Section 362) -- the foundational protection that halts all creditor action against the debtor when bankruptcy is filed. The codebtor stay extends this concept to third parties on consumer debts.

Relief from Stay (Section 362(d)) -- the process creditors use to lift the automatic stay as to the debtor. Similar in concept to lifting the codebtor stay under Section 1301(c), but with different grounds and standards.

Chapter 13 Plan -- the repayment plan that determines how creditors are paid over the life of the case. The plan's treatment of codebtor claims directly affects the cosigner's exposure.
